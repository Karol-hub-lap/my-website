The Woman King: Reclaiming African Warrior Narratives

Amina Diallo

June 15, 2023 · 4 min read

The 2022 historical epic The Woman King, directed by Gina Prince-Bythewood and starring Viola Davis, has been hailed as a groundbreaking moment for African representation in Hollywood. The film tells the story of the Agojie, the all-female warrior unit that protected the West African kingdom of Dahomey during the 17th to 19th centuries.

Historical Context

Dahomey (present-day Benin) was known for its military prowess and complex social structures. The Agojie, often called "Dahomey Amazons" by European observers, were an elite fighting force that played a crucial role in the kingdom's expansion and defense.

While the film takes creative liberties with specific events, it captures the essence of these warriors' significance. The production team consulted with historians and descendants of the Agojie to ensure cultural authenticity in costumes, language (Fon), and combat styles.

Cultural Significance

The Woman King challenges the Western narrative that has often portrayed African societies as passive victims of the transatlantic slave trade. Instead, it presents a nuanced view where African kingdoms were complex political entities with agency.

The film's focus on female warriors also subverts traditional gender roles in historical epics, which typically center male protagonists. This aligns with oral traditions that celebrate the Agojie's strength and discipline.

Critical Analysis

While the film has been praised for its action sequences and performances (particularly Viola Davis as General Nanisca), some scholars have criticized its handling of Dahomey's involvement in the slave trade. The kingdom did participate in the trade before eventually banning it, a complexity the film simplifies for narrative purposes.

Nevertheless, The Woman King represents an important step in reclaiming African historical narratives from Eurocentric perspectives. Its commercial success ($94 million worldwide) demonstrates audience appetite for such stories.

About AfriLens Review

Our Mission

AfriLens Review was founded in 2025 with the goal of providing critical perspectives on African media through the lens of our rich cultural heritage, legal traditions, and contemporary development challenges. We believe that thoughtful media criticism can contribute to a more nuanced understanding of Africa's place in global cultural production.

Our Vision

We envision a world where African narratives are analyzed with the same depth and respect as Western media, where our cultural contexts inform critical frameworks, and where African audiences see themselves authentically represented in media criticism.

About the Founder
